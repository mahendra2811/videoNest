# VideoNest Encoding Strategy: Technical Validation & Improvement Plan

## TL;DR
- **Over-provision for YouTube, match-and-pad for everything else.** YouTube's re-encoder rewards a high-bitrate source — controlled VMAF tests show ~60 Mbps H.265/AV1 is the practical quality ceiling, essentially matching a near-lossless ~700 Mbps master, and uploading 4K/1440p improves even the 1080p stream. But Instagram, WhatsApp, and Facebook Story/Reels cap at 1080p with speed-tuned (low-quality) downscalers, so feeding them 4K or 20+ Mbps makes their encoder work *harder* and produces *more* artifacts. Match 1080p exactly with a moderate over-provision (~10–16 Mbps).
- **Keep H.264 High/yuv420p as the universal default; add AV1/VP9/HEVC only as an opt-in "YouTube best quality" path.** WebCodecs hardware H.264 is the correct primary encoder for a mobile-first on-device tool — modern hardware encoders now match or beat libx264-veryfast on quality, run ~8× faster, and use ~10× less power. Keep the libx264 (ffmpeg.wasm) fallback, but software x264 is not worth making the default, and multi-threaded ffmpeg.wasm is not worth adopting (it's broken on Chromium).
- **Your table is mostly right but has fixable errors:** WhatsApp Status is **16 MB** (not 15), Instagram Reels can now run to 3 min for many accounts, YouTube Shorts is 3 min / 1080p-max (since Oct 15 2024), and you should **drop loudness normalization by default** — all these platforms normalize themselves, so applying −14 LUFS yourself is at best redundant and at worst triggers double-limiting.

## Key Findings

### A. The central question: match vs over-provision
The decisive insight is that **the answer is platform-dependent and asymmetric**, not universal:

- **YouTube (long-form + Shorts): over-provision.** Independent VMAF testing by Zeb Gardner (zebgardner.com, "2026 Update, Best Upload Settings for YouTube," updated Jan 6 2026) uploaded encodes to YouTube, re-downloaded them via yt-dlp, and scored them against a near-lossless DNxHR master. **AV1 at 60 Mbps scored VMAF 99.67; H.265 60 Mbps scored 99.75** — statistically tied with the ~700 Mbps master (99.92). Critically, **a native-1080p upload scored *worst* (VMAF 97.32, SSIM 89.12)** because it triggers YouTube's inferior H.264 1080p path rather than the VP9/AV1 ladder that 4K/1440p uploads unlock. An earlier controlled test (andsobegins.com, 2020) found uploading content as 4K instead of 1080p lifted average VMAF from 68 to 92. Gardner's 2022 test also showed that for high-bitrate uploads "all the quality loss happens on YouTube's end," while low-bitrate uploads are passed through unchanged ("garbage in, garbage out") — you cannot recover quality by under-provisioning.
- **Diminishing-returns ceiling:** ~60 Mbps for 4K on YouTube (AV1 60 Mbps actually beat AV1 100 Mbps); for 1080p, roughly 12–16 Mbps. YouTube's published bitrates are **floors, not ceilings**: 1080p SDR 30fps = 8 Mbps / 60fps = 12 Mbps; 4K SDR 30fps = 35–45 Mbps / 60fps = 53–85 Mbps (YouTube official guidance). RealNetworks/OTTVerse establish VMAF >95 as "subjectively indistinguishable from source" — past that you waste bits.
- **Instagram Reels/Stories, WhatsApp, Facebook Story: match, don't over-provision.** These cap delivery at 1080p and re-encode to a low target (Instagram's encoder reportedly targets ~3.5 Mbps regardless of input). Uploading 4K forces their speed-tuned downscaler to do the resize, introducing artifacts and banding that would not appear if you downscaled to exactly 1080×1920 yourself with a good filter (Lanczos). **Caveat:** no rigorous controlled VMAF study exists for Instagram/TikTok — this is consistent vendor/practitioner guidance, not peer-reviewed measurement, and should be flagged as such in any internal doc.

**Rate-control verdict — your constrained-VBR (CRF + maxrate) approach is correct** and matches Streaming Learning Center (Jan Ozer) and broad industry practice:
- For short clips (the bulk of VideoNest use), **single-pass vs two-pass VBR shows "very little difference"** (Streaming Learning Center). Two-pass is only worth it for precise size targeting on longer clips — i.e., your WhatsApp size-budget path.
- **Capped CRF** (`-crf X -maxrate Y -bufsize 2Y`) is "the right answer for most modern streaming use cases — quality target with a hard ceiling" (Forasoft, 2026), and is essentially what your ffmpeg path already does. Per NETINT (citing Netflix's video-algorithms team), "Capped CRF can achieve up to 30% bitrate savings compared to CBR while maintaining equivalent perceptual quality"; NETINT's own Quadra 4K CRF-23 test hit ~45% savings over CBR at VMAF ~95.
- **110% constrained VBR (not 200%)** is Ozer's recommendation; bufsize = 2× maxrate is the sane default — your `-maxrate <b> -bufsize <2b>` matches this. A CRF and 2-pass encode landing at the same bitrate are "identical in quality" (slhck), so the only reason to add 2-pass is WhatsApp's file-size precision.

**GOP / B-frames for re-upload survival:** YouTube's official spec (support.google.com/youtube/answer/1722171) calls verbatim for "**Closed GOP. GOP of half the frame rate**," plus 2 consecutive B-frames, CABAC, High Profile, and 4:2:0 chroma. Your 2-second closed GOP (keyint = 2×fps) is *more conservative* than YouTube's ~0.5s recommendation — fine and safe, but for the YouTube profile specifically, consider matching ~1s, since shorter GOPs and B-frames give the platform's encoder cleaner reference points. Your H.264 High/yuv420p closed-GOP choice is otherwise correct.

### B. Codec & encoder choice
- **H.264 High remains the safe universal default** — universally accepted, hardware-decoded everywhere, and the only combination that "uploaded without a single failure" in cross-platform testing (fone.tips, 18-upload test on iOS/Android, 2026). Switching from H.265/VP9 to H.264 "fixed every rejection."
- **Offer AV1/VP9/HEVC only as an opt-in YouTube path.** YouTube transcodes everything to AV1/VP9 for playback anyway; uploading AV1 (HW-permitting) or H.265 at high bitrate is the top-quality YouTube path (VMAF 99.67–99.75). But WebCodecs AV1 encode is patchy, HEVC encode is essentially Safari-only, and VP9 *upload* scored *worse* than H.264/H.265 in Gardner's tests. So: keep H.264 default; add an "AV1/H.265 for YouTube" toggle gated on `VideoEncoder.isConfigSupported`.
- **WebCodecs hardware H.264 vs libx264 software — keep WebCodecs primary.** The old "hardware is lower quality" gap has largely closed: NVIDIA's Turing-NVENC benchmarks show "same or slightly better visual quality compared to libx264 in high quality mode," and Wowza found modern hardware now "exceeds that available with x264/veryfast." Your ffmpeg.wasm fallback runs libx264 at `-preset veryfast` (the realistic WASM ceiling), so hardware H.264 is competitive *and* dramatically faster — WebCodecs+Mediabunny ~200 fps vs ffmpeg.wasm ~25 fps for 1080p H.264 on a 2018 MacBook Pro (Dayverse benchmark), at ~10× lower power. For a mobile-first tool this is decisive. The academic RD penalty of hardware encoders (arXiv 2511.18686, Nov 2025) is real but small and outweighed by battery/speed on phones.
- **Multi-threaded ffmpeg.wasm — don't bother.** It offers ~2× over single-thread but "consumes a lot more memory and CPU," and **multithreaded ffmpeg.wasm is broken on Chromium-based browsers (works only on Firefox)** per multiple GitHub issues (#530, #597) — the opposite of your user base. Since WebCodecs is your fast path and ffmpeg.wasm is only a fallback, keep single-thread for reliability and lower init overhead.

### C. Pre-processing filters
- **Light pre-processing is a double-edged sword — default it OFF.** Consensus (Wondershare/Repairit, Smartupworld, encode.moe) is that *clean* source compresses better, but **over-sharpening and aggressive denoise backfire**: they "amplify edges and flatten textures, making artifacts easier to see" and create ringing/halos that the platform encoder then preserves and amplifies. A *mild* denoise on genuinely noisy/grainy footage helps (noise is expensive to encode and steals the platform's fixed bitrate budget); sharpening to "survive" recompression generally hurts. Recommendation: no sharpening by default; optional light denoise (`hqdn3d`) only when input is detectably noisy.
- **Aspect mismatch — your blur-pad is the right default but is not free.** Blur-pad (blurred cover + sharp contained source) is the most engagement-friendly option and is what CapCut/InShot do, and platforms penalize letterboxed/black-bar content in Reels/Shorts/Stories. But both blur-pad and letterbox consume bitrate that the platform's fixed budget then steals from your real content. Keep blur-pad default for vertical platforms; offer crop-to-fill as a user option.
- **HDR→SDR, 10-bit→8-bit, BT.709, rotation — your biggest reliability gap.** WebCodecs has poor HDR support: 10-bit `yuv420p10le` VideoFrames return `null` format and can't be read via `copyTo` (w3c/webcodecs discussions #631, #47), and browser HDR tone-mapping requires a manual WebGPU/WebGL shader path. **Robust approach:** detect BT.2020/PQ/HLG via the Mediabunny probe → route HDR to a tone-map step (WebGPU shader, or ffmpeg.wasm `zscale`+`tonemap` fallback) → output BT.709 8-bit. YouTube itself converts BT.2020-without-HDR-transfer to BT.709 8-bit to avoid banding, so SDR-ize before upload for all SDR-only platforms. **Rotation:** WebCodecs does **not** auto-apply rotation metadata — iPhone clips with a 90° flag decode unrotated (w3c/webcodecs #351; Remotion docs confirm `VideoDecoder` won't rotate for you). You must read the track rotation (Mediabunny exposes it) and apply it on the OffscreenCanvas compositor. This is a common silent bug — verify your compositor handles it.

### D. Audio
- **Drop per-platform loudness normalization by default. All seven targets normalize loudness server-side.** Per Critical Listening Lab, "YouTube applies loudness normalization… content louder than approximately −14 LUFS is turned down; content quieter is left at its original level." Undetectr's 2026 reference table: "Spotify, YouTube, Tidal, Amazon, TikTok, and Instagram all normalize to −14. Apple Music and Facebook use −16 LUFS." (Note: TikTok in-feed playback is widely reported *not* to normalize — but TikTok isn't a VideoNest target.) If you normalize to −14 yourself *and* the platform normalizes, the work is redundant; if you push louder than target, the platform applies limiting that crushes dynamics. Your current behavior (pass through at source loudness) is correct. Offer an opt-in "normalize quiet sources to −14 LUFS" toggle using `loudnorm=I=-14:TP=-1.5:LRA=11`; do not default it on.
- **AAC bitrate per platform:** AAC-LC 48 kHz everywhere is correct. YouTube's official spec recommends "128 kbps for mono, **384 kbps for stereo**, 512 kbps for 5.1" for the *source* (YouTube then streams ~160 kbps Opus to viewers) — so raise YouTube long-form/Shorts source audio to 256–384 kbps. 128 kbps is the fine floor for short-form vertical platforms. **Mono** is acceptable for voice-only WhatsApp Status clips to save the tight 16 MB budget for video.

### F. Centralization & architecture
- **Your single data-driven profile registry is the correct pattern — keep it.** There is **no authoritative open, maintained machine-readable database of social-platform encoding specs** to pull from. The "social media specs" guides (Sprout Social, Vista Social, etc.) are human-readable marketing content that drifts and disagrees; the only source with a true engineering spec is YouTube's own help doc. API aggregators (Ayrshare, Zernio, EmbedSocial) normalize *posting*, not *encoding params*. Hand-maintaining your registry — anchored to YouTube's official doc plus periodic re-verification — is right. Add a `lastVerified` date + source-URL field per platform.
- **Server-tier break-even:** Keep dormant by default (correct for privacy/cost). On-device WebCodecs handles up to ~1080p short-to-medium clips fast and free. The server case (native FFmpeg, 2-pass, AV1) is specifically **4K + long-form + AV1/HEVC** where WebCodecs lacks the encoder or the device OOMs. Pricing makes this trivial: Coconut $0.015/output-min ("a 10-minute video transcoded into 2 output formats = 20 output minutes = $0.30"), Mux ~$0.07/encode-min with AI per-title encoding, Cloudinary credit-based ($89+/mo). Gate any server use behind explicit user opt-in to preserve the no-upload privacy promise.

### G. Reliability / stability
- **OOM on large/4K/long:** ffmpeg.wasm uses >2 GB for 1080p; 4K can blow the WASM heap. Your OOM→lower-res-retry→ffmpeg-fallback ladder is good. Add decode→encode pipeline throttling (Remotion's `waitForQueueToBeLessThan` pattern) so frames don't pile up in front of the encoder and balloon memory.
- **VFR source:** Variable-frame-rate (common in screen recordings) makes Instagram uploads "fail near the end." Force constant frame rate on output — your fixed-fps GOP implies this; confirm it.
- **Rotation / 10-bit / HDR:** see C — must apply rotation manually; `copyTo` fails on null-format 10-bit frames, so detect and route to the ffmpeg.wasm/WebGPU path.
- **Unusual codecs (ProRes/DNxHR/MKV):** WebCodecs can't decode these; fall back to ffmpeg.wasm for demux/decode then re-feed. Your fallback covers this.
- **Cancellation:** WebCodecs needs explicit `encoder.close()`/`decoder.close()` plus queue drain; ensure the cancel path flushes so leaked VideoFrames (each holding GPU memory) don't accumulate.

## Caveats

The YouTube findings *are* backed by reproducible VMAF tests (Zeb Gardner), but that is an n=1 small-channel study that conflates Handbrake + YouTube loss and computes VMAF against a DNxHR master (inflating absolute scores toward ~99). Treat the YouTube↔everything-else asymmetry as well-supported in *direction*, less so in exact numbers.
- **Platform specs drift constantly.** Every number reflects 2024–2026 sources; re-verify against platform help docs quarterly. Recent churn: Instagram's 3-min Reels, YouTube Shorts' 3-min limit (Oct 15 2024), Facebook's "Video" tab renamed to "Reels" (June 2025).
- **Many "best settings 2026" articles are AI/SEO content** paraphrasing a small set of primary sources (notably Zeb Gardner and YouTube's own doc). Findings here are anchored to primary sources where possible, with derivative ones flagged.
- **WebCodecs support is still maturing** — Safari is partial, Firefox lags, and HDR/10-bit paths are incomplete across all browsers as of mid-2026. The ffmpeg.wasm fallback remains necessary, not optional.
